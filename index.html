Shapes & sizes
Cigars are measured by length (inches) and ring gauge (64ths of an inch). Shape affects draw speed, burn rate, and flavor development.
Strength guide
Strength refers to nicotine content and body — not flavor intensity. Always eat before smoking a full-strength cigar.

Humidor Care
A well-maintained humidor is the difference between a cigar that sings and one that disappoints. These are the fundamentals every collector should know.
Humidity
65–70% RH
Ideal
The sweet spot for most premium cigars. Preserves oils, maintains draw, and allows slow graceful aging. Most collectors aim for 68–69%.
62–65% RH
Drier Storage
Preferred by many aficionados for long-term aging. A drier environment draws out more complexity over years. Cigars burn slower and cooler at this range.
70–72% RH
Upper Limit
Acceptable for short-term storage but risky long-term. Above 72% risks mold, tobacco beetle eggs hatching, and wrapper damage. Never exceed 75%.
Temperature
65–70°F / 18–21°C
Ideal
Promotes slow, even aging. At 70°F / 21°C you are right at the threshold where tobacco beetles become active — staying just below is important.
Above 72°F / 22°C
Danger Zone
Tobacco beetles hatch above 72°F and will tunnel through your entire collection. If temperatures spike, quarantine new cigars in a sealed bag in the freezer for 72 hours before adding to your humidor.
Below 60°F / 15°C
Too Cold
Very cold temperatures stall aging and can cause the wrapper to crack. Cigars become brittle and may lose essential oils. Not recommended for long-term storage.
Types of Humidors
Desktop Humidor
The classic. A wooden box — typically Spanish cedar lined — that sits on a desk or shelf. Holds 25 to 150 cigars. The Spanish cedar interior absorbs and releases moisture naturally and repels tobacco beetles. Best for everyday collections.
Cabinet Humidor
A freestanding cabinet that holds hundreds to thousands of cigars. Often temperature-controlled as well as humidity-controlled. Used by serious collectors and retailers. Requires a more powerful humidification system.
Coolidor
A beverage cooler repurposed as a humidor. Airtight seal makes it extremely efficient at holding humidity. Holds hundreds of cigars at a fraction of the cost of a cabinet humidor. Line the inside with Spanish cedar sheets for best results.
Tupperdor
An airtight plastic container — such as a Tupperware or Sistema box — used as a budget humidor. Surprisingly effective due to the airtight seal. Great for beginners or travel storage. Add a small Boveda pack and you are ready to go.
Travel Humidor
A compact hard case designed to carry 3 to 10 cigars safely. Usually crushproof and waterproof. Essential for protecting cigars in transit. Not suitable for long-term storage but perfect for your bag or carry-on.
How to Set Up a New Humidor
1
Season the wood
Place a small dish of distilled water or a damp sponge inside the empty humidor. Close the lid and leave for 24–48 hours. This slowly hydrates the Spanish cedar so it does not absorb all the moisture from your cigars when you first load it. Never use tap water — minerals cause residue and mold.
2
Calibrate your hygrometer
Place your hygrometer in a sealed bag with a damp paper towel for 1 hour. It should read 96–100% RH. If it reads differently, note the offset and apply it when reading your humidor. Digital hygrometers are more accurate than analog and cost under $15.
3
Install your humidification device
Place your chosen humidification source and your hygrometer inside. Close the lid. Check after 24 hours — you want to see 65–70% RH before adding cigars. If it is still too low, repeat the seasoning step.
4
Load your cigars gradually
Do not fill a new humidor all at once. Add cigars in batches over several days, monitoring the humidity after each addition. A humidor full of dry cigars will quickly absorb moisture and drop your RH. Gradual loading keeps everything stable.
5
Rotate your cigars monthly
Swap top and bottom rows once a month to ensure even humidity distribution. Cigars closest to the humidification source age faster. Rotation gives every cigar equal exposure and prevents uneven aging.
Humidity & Temperature Control Options
Boveda Packs ⭐ Most Recommended
Two-way humidity control packets that both add and absorb moisture to maintain a precise RH — available in 62%, 65%, 69%, and 72%. Simply place inside your humidor and replace every 2–3 months. Zero maintenance, extremely reliable, and affordable. The gold standard for desktop and tupperdor use.
Electronic Humidifier
A small fan-powered device that pumps humidity into your humidor at a set level. Best for larger collections and cabinet humidors. Requires refilling with distilled water every 2–4 weeks. Cigar Oasis is the most respected brand. More consistent than passive options for large collections.
Propylene Glycol Solution
A 50/50 mix of propylene glycol and distilled water used to fill foam or crystal humidification devices. Naturally self-regulates to approximately 70% RH. Inexpensive and effective for smaller humidors. Requires refilling monthly and occasional cleaning to prevent mold.
Gel Crystals / Beads
Silica gel crystals or beads that can be charged to a specific humidity level. They both absorb excess moisture and release moisture when levels drop. Long-lasting, rechargeable, and visually indicate when they need attention by changing color. A good middle ground between Boveda and electronic options.
Wine Cooler / Wineador
A thermoelectric wine cooler repurposed as a climate-controlled humidor. Maintains both temperature and humidity when paired with an electronic humidifier. Holds 150 to 300+ cigars. The ultimate upgrade for the serious collector — controls both humidity and temperature simultaneously and keeps beetles completely at bay.
Leaves & Wrappers
The wrapper is the first thing you see and the last leaf applied — yet it contributes up to 60% of a cigar's flavor. Understanding leaf types transforms how you choose and appreciate a cigar.
Wrapper Colors
Claro — Very Light
Shade-grown under cloth to prevent chlorophyll development. Extremely light tan color. Mild, smooth, and creamy flavor. Less common today but beloved by those who prefer a delicate smoke. Associated with Connecticut Shade wrappers.
Colorado Claro — Light Brown
A light to medium brown with a slight golden hue. Balanced and approachable — mild to medium strength with notes of cream, cedar, and light spice. Excellent for beginners. Ecuador Connecticut wrappers fall here.
Colorado — Medium Brown
The most common wrapper shade. Rich reddish-brown with complex flavors — cedar, leather, earth, and subtle sweetness. Medium body, excellent balance. Found on many Honduran and Dominican cigars.
Colorado Maduro — Dark Brown
A deep, rich brown that transitions toward maduro. Fuller body with prominent chocolate, espresso, and dried fruit notes. The wrapper has been fermented longer than a colorado but not as long as a true maduro.
Maduro — Very Dark Brown to Black
Fermented for months at high temperature and pressure. The process breaks down oils into sugars, creating natural sweetness alongside deep chocolate, coffee, and earth notes. Counter-intuitively, a true maduro is not necessarily stronger — it is richer and sweeter. One of the most beloved wrapper styles.
Candela — Green
Flash-cured at high heat to lock in chlorophyll, producing a distinctive green wrapper. Very mild, grassy, and slightly sweet. Rarely seen today but popular in the 1960s. An acquired taste that cigar historians appreciate.
Leaf Priming — Where the Leaf Grows
Tobacco leaves are harvested in passes called primings from the bottom of the plant upward. Position on the plant determines character.
Volado — Bottom Leaves
Mild, burns easily. Used as filler for combustion.
Mild
Seco — Middle Leaves
Balanced flavor and aroma. The backbone of most blends.
Medium
Ligero — Top Leaves
Most sun exposure — thick, oily, full-bodied. Adds power and complexity.
Full
Medio Tiempo — Crown Leaves
Rarest and most prized. Found only on the top two leaves of certain plants. Exceptional complexity. Very scarce.
Rare
Binder & Filler
The Binder
A single leaf that wraps around the filler bunch to hold it together before the wrapper is applied. The binder contributes significantly to the cigar's draw and can add its own flavor notes. Often a thicker, coarser leaf than the wrapper.
The Filler
A blend of 3 to 5 different leaves rolled together at the core. This is where the master blender's art lives — the specific combination of volado, seco, and ligero leaves determines the cigar's flavor arc from first third to final third. Most filler blends use leaves from multiple countries.
Countries of Origin
Tobacco's character is inseparable from the soil it grows in. Each region produces leaves with a distinct personality shaped by climate, altitude, and centuries of tradition.
Cuba
Vuelta Abajo
The origin of everything. The Vuelta Abajo region in Pinar del Río is considered the finest tobacco-growing land on earth — a perfect combination of red clay soil, subtropical climate, and centuries of expertise. Cuban tobacco is known for its complex earthiness, subtle sweetness, and a finish that evolves over the length of the smoke. Cohiba, Montecristo, Partagás, Romeo y Julieta, and H. Upmann define the canon. Flavor profile: earth, cedar, leather, cream, subtle spice.
Nicaragua
Jalapa · Estelí · Ometepe
The new world power in premium cigars. Nicaragua's volcanic soil and diverse microclimates produce tobacco with remarkable complexity and body — often compared favorably to Cuba. The Jalapa valley is known for sweeter, more refined leaves while Estelí produces the bold, peppery, full-bodied tobacco that defines Nicaraguan strength. Padron, Oliva, AJ Fernandez, and Perdomo are the great names. Flavor profile: pepper, dark chocolate, earth, leather, coffee, cedar.
Honduras
Jamastran Valley
The Jamastran Valley has some of the most fertile tobacco soil in Central America. Honduran tobacco tends toward a rich, hearty medium-full body with natural sweetness and excellent combustion properties. Often used as binder or filler in blends from multiple factories. Rocky Patel, Camacho, and Alec Bradley showcase Honduran tobacco at its best. Flavor profile: sweet wood, nuts, earth, mild pepper, honey.
Dominican Republic
Cibao Valley
The Dominican Republic became the center of premium cigar production after the Cuban embargo and remains one of the world's most important sources. Dominican tobacco is celebrated for its smoothness, elegance, and refined character. The Cibao Valley produces exceptionally light and aromatic leaves. Arturo Fuente, Davidoff, and La Gloria Cubana are rooted here. Flavor profile: cream, cedar, floral, light spice, nuts.
Ecuador
Under the Cloud Forest
Ecuador's unique natural cloud cover at high altitude creates a permanent diffused light canopy — mimicking shade-grown conditions without artificial cloth. This produces extraordinarily silky, smooth wrapper leaves with minimal veins. Ecuador Connecticut wrappers are among the most prized in the world, used by factories from Nicaragua to the Dominican Republic. Flavor profile: cream, vanilla, cedar, light earth, floral.
Connecticut, USA
Connecticut Valley
Connecticut Shade-grown tobacco is one of the most important wrapper varieties in the world. Grown under white cloth tents along the Connecticut River Valley, the leaves develop with delicate texture and subtle flavor. Connecticut Broadleaf is darker and fuller, used for maduro-style wrappers. General Cigar's Connecticut Shade is the benchmark. Flavor profile: cream, hay, light wood, mild sweetness.
Brazil
Bahia · Arapiraca
Brazilian tobacco — particularly from the Bahia and Arapiraca regions — is prized for its natural sweetness and dark, rustic wrappers. Brazilian maduro leaves ferment beautifully and are used in some of the world's finest maduro blends. The tobacco has a raw, earthy character that adds depth to complex filler blends. Flavor profile: molasses, dark earth, leather, raisin, dark chocolate.
Costa Rica
Cartago · San José
Costa Rican tobacco benefits from rich volcanic soil and a consistent tropical climate. Though a smaller producer than its Central American neighbors, Costa Rica grows wrapper and filler leaves prized for their natural sweetness and smooth combustion. The EL Rey del Mundo and Café Toraño brands have showcased Costa Rican leaf to great effect. Flavor profile: sweet wood, cream, mild earth, light spice, toast.
Indonesia
Sumatra · Java · Besuki
Indonesia is one of the world's most important tobacco-producing countries, with three distinct growing regions each producing unique leaf. Sumatra wrapper is legendary — thin, silky, and beautifully veined with a characteristic earthy sweetness that has been prized by European cigar makers for centuries. Java and Besuki leaves are commonly used as binders in Dutch and German-style cigars. Flavor profile: earth, dark spice, cocoa, aged wood, subtle sweetness.
Mexico
San Andrés Valley
The San Andrés Valley in Veracruz is Mexico's premium tobacco heartland. San Andrés Maduro wrapper is one of the most celebrated maduro leaves in the world — dark, oily, naturally sweet, and exceptionally smooth when fermented properly. It has been used by master blenders from Carlos Toraño to Arturo Fuente as the foundation for iconic maduro blends. Flavor profile: dark chocolate, espresso, earth, dried fruit, natural sweetness.
Cameroon
Central Africa
Cameroon wrapper is one of the most distinctive and sought-after in the premium cigar world. Grown in the rich equatorial soil of Central Africa, it produces a toothy, slightly rough-textured leaf with a unique flavor profile found nowhere else. Cameroon tobacco was the foundation of the original Davidoff line and remains deeply associated with the golden era of Dominican cigar making. Production is limited, making authentic Cameroon wrappers increasingly rare and valuable. Flavor profile: cedar, hay, subtle sweetness, mild pepper, barnyard, distinctive earthiness.

What is Torcida

Torcida is your AI-powered cigar companion — built for everyone from the first-time smoker to the seasoned collector. Search any cigar for rich tasting notes and pairing recommendations, track your humidor, log every smoke, and explore the craft through a beautifully designed reference guide.

One app. Every part of the ritual.

Cigar Culture is for Everyone

You don't need twenty years of experience to deserve a seat at the table. All you need is curiosity and a willingness to slow down. Torcida was built to make the cigar world as welcoming to the newcomer as it is rewarding to the aficionado.

"In the cigar lounge, the corner office and the first job blur together. The smoke is the same. The pause is the same. The moment is the same."
— The Torcida Philosophy
Why We Are Called Torcida

Torcida is the feminine past participle of "to roll" in Spanish and Portuguese — a deliberate tribute to the women whose skilled hands have built the premium cigar industry for generations. Their names are rarely on the bands, but without them, there is no cigar. In Portuguese, torcida also means a passionate, devoted fan base — which is exactly what we are building.

In Portuguese, torcida also means a passionate fan base — the devoted community that shows up with energy, loyalty, and love. That is exactly what we hope Torcida the app becomes: a community of passionate people who show up for the craft, for each other, and for the culture.

The Journal

A journal is the master blender's logbook — the private journal where every blend experiment, every leaf combination, every aging note, and every tasting observation is recorded by hand. The great factories of Cuba, Nicaragua, and Honduras have kept journals for generations. They are the accumulated wisdom of the craft.

When you log a smoke in Torcida, you are keeping your own journal. Your tasting notes, your pairings, your ratings — they are your record of the journey. Every entry honors the tradition of those who came before you.
