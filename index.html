Shapes & Sizes
Cigars are measured by length (inches) and ring gauge (64ths of an inch). Shape affects draw speed, burn rate, and flavor development.
Strength Guide
Strength refers to nicotine content and body — not flavor intensity. Always eat before smoking a full-strength cigar.
Wrapper color guide
Types of Cuts
The cap cut opens the cigar's head for draw. A clean, precise cut is one of the most important things you can do for your smoke — a poor cut causes unraveling, tight draws, and uneven burns.
Cutting technique
How much to cut
Remove only 1–2mm from the cap — just enough to open the draw. Cutting too deep past the shoulder removes the cap entirely and causes the wrapper to unravel. Look for the cap line (a faint ring where the wrapper ends) and cut just above it.
Blade sharpness matters
A dull cutter crushes and tears the cap instead of slicing it cleanly. This bruises the tobacco, tightens the draw, and causes the wrapper to crack or peel. A quality guillotine cutter replaced every 1–2 years will serve you better than an expensive but dull one.
Single vs. double blade
Double-bladed guillotines cut from both sides simultaneously, reducing lateral stress on the cap and producing a cleaner edge. Single-blade cutters are perfectly fine but require a confident, swift motion — hesitation causes compression and tearing.
Matching cut to ring gauge
Larger ring gauge cigars (50+) draw more air by default — a V-cut or straight cut works well. Smaller ring gauges (38–46) can become overly restrictive — use a guillotine with a wide opening, not a punch, which can make the draw too tight on a thin cigar.
Torpedo & figurado tips
Tapered heads (torpedo, belicoso, pyramid) concentrate flavor. Cut at the widest point of the taper — about 1/4 inch from the tip — to preserve the tapered draw experience while still opening the cigar fully. Never cut all the way to the shoulder on a figurado.
Cut comparison
Guillotine (Straight Cut)
Best all-around cut. Open, generous draw. Works on all ring gauges. Most forgiving for beginners.
Universal
V-Cut (Wedge / Cat's Eye)
Cuts a V-shaped wedge into the cap. Concentrates smoke through a narrow channel, intensifying flavors. Excellent on larger ring gauges (50+). Adds complexity and richness.
Flavor+
Punch Cut
A circular punch bores a small hole into the cap rather than removing it. Keeps the cap intact. Cooler, slower smoke. Reduces draw slightly — ideal for medium/large ring gauges. Avoid on small ring sizes.
Cooler smoke
Shuriken / Multi-Blade
Multiple small cuts radiating from the center. Creates multiple draw channels. Not widely used but produces a unique draw with less resistance. Rare — mostly found among enthusiasts who experiment with draw.
Specialist
How to Light a Cigar
Lighting is a ritual. Done properly it ensures an even, flavorful smoke from first draw to the final third. Never rush it.

Humidor Care
A well-maintained humidor is the difference between a cigar that sings and one that disappoints. Cigars are living things — they continue to ferment and evolve in storage, and the environment you provide determines whether that evolution improves or ruins them.
Humidity levels
65–70% RH
Ideal
The sweet spot for most premium cigars. Preserves essential oils, maintains draw resistance, prevents cracking, and allows slow graceful aging. Most manufacturers store at this range.
62–65% RH
Aging Storage
Preferred by many aficionados for long-term aging. Lower humidity slows fermentation and produces a slower, cooler burn. Cuban cigars in particular benefit from this range after the initial bloom period.
70–72% RH
Upper Limit
Above 72% risks mold growth (white bloom vs. blue-green mold — learn the difference), tobacco beetle eggs hatching, and wrapper splitting. Never exceed 75% under any circumstances.
Below 60% RH
Too Dry
Cigars dry out rapidly below 60%. Wrappers crack and flake, oils evaporate, harshness increases, and the cigar burns hot and fast. Dry cigars can be rehydrated slowly over weeks, but rapid re-humidification splits wrappers.
Temperature
65–70°F (18–21°C)
Ideal
Cool and stable. Below 70°F keeps tobacco beetles dormant — their eggs are present in virtually all premium tobacco and hatch above 72°F, devastating an entire collection.
Above 72°F (22°C)
Beetle Risk
Tobacco beetle eggs begin hatching above 72°F. If you suspect infestation (tiny holes in the wrapper, fine tobacco dust inside the humidor), quarantine all cigars immediately and freeze them at 0°F for 72 hours before returning to the humidor.
Seasoning a new humidor
Why seasoning matters
New Spanish cedar absorbs massive amounts of moisture. An unseasoned humidor will rob humidity from your cigars, causing them to dry out even with an active humidifier. Proper seasoning saturates the wood so it maintains rather than consumes humidity.
The wipe method (recommended)
Dampen a clean cloth or sponge with distilled water only — never tap water, which contains minerals that damage wood and humidifier elements. Wipe all interior cedar surfaces lightly. Place a small dish of distilled water inside, close the lid, and wait 24–48 hours. Check humidity — if it reads 70%+ consistently, the humidor is ready.
The slow method (safest)
Place an activated humidifier and a digital hygrometer inside without any cigars. Close the lid for 2 weeks. Let the cedar absorb moisture gradually. This prevents warping, which can occur if unseasoned wood is exposed to rapid moisture introduction. After 2 weeks, readings should stabilize — then add cigars.
Distilled water only
Tap water contains chlorine, minerals, and bacteria that leave residue, clog humidifier elements, promote mold, and impart off-flavors to tobacco. Always use distilled or purified water. Propylene glycol (PG) solution in a 50/50 mix with distilled water is the gold standard for passive humidifiers — it self-regulates at approximately 70% RH.
Ongoing maintenance
Digital hygrometer — non-negotiable
Analog hygrometers that ship with most humidors are notoriously inaccurate — often 10–15% off. A quality digital hygrometer (Boveda Caliber, Xikar, or similar) costs $20–40 and pays for itself immediately by preventing a ruined collection.
Rotation
Rotate cigars every 3–6 months. Cigars near the humidifier or top of the stack receive more moisture than those at the bottom. Rotation ensures even aging across your collection and prevents any single cigar from becoming over- or under-humidified.
Boveda packs — the modern standard
Two-way humidity control packets that both add and remove moisture to maintain a precise RH level (58%, 62%, 65%, 69%, 72% available). Replace when the pack becomes fully hardened — typically every 2–4 months depending on traffic and seal quality. Never use both a Boveda pack and a traditional humidifier simultaneously.
Quarantine new cigars
Always quarantine new cigars in a separate container for 2 weeks before adding them to your main humidor. New arrivals may carry pests, mold, or dramatically different humidity levels that can destabilize your existing collection.
Plume vs. mold — know the difference
Plume (bloom) is a white crystalline powder that forms on well-aged cigars as oils migrate to the surface — it brushes off cleanly and is a sign of good aging. Mold is fuzzy, circular, and usually blue-green — it spreads and must be addressed immediately by removing affected cigars, wiping surfaces with a 50/50 water-isopropyl cloth, and lowering humidity.
Humidor types
Desktop humidor
The classic. Spanish cedar-lined wood box with a Spanish cedar tray. Ideal for 25–200 cigars. Requires manual monitoring and maintenance. Best for collections you smoke regularly.
Wineador (wine cooler conversion)
A wine cooler retrofitted with Spanish cedar shelving and Boveda packs or electronic humidifier. Holds hundreds to thousands of cigars with stable temperature control. The gold standard for serious collectors. More setup required but near-perfect climate control.
Tupperdor
An airtight plastic container (like Tupperware or Sterilite) with Boveda packs. Airtight seal maintains humidity with minimal evaporation — often outperforms expensive wooden humidors. Lacks the aesthetic but excels at preservation. Ideal for travel and backup storage.
Cabinet humidor
Full-sized furniture piece holding thousands of cigars. Glass doors, electronic humidification and temperature systems. For dedicated walk-in collections or cigar lounges. Significant investment but commercial-grade climate control.
Leaves & Wrappers
The wrapper leaf contributes up to 60% of a cigar's total flavor. It is the most visually prominent leaf and the most expensive to grow. Understanding leaf types, their cultivation, and fermentation transforms how you choose and experience a cigar.
Anatomy of a cigar
Wrapper (Capa)
The outermost leaf. Must be blemish-free, silky in texture, and oily. Contributes the most to the visual impression and a significant portion of the flavor — particularly sweetness, spice, and overall character. Grown under shade cloth to produce fine-veined, elastic leaves.
Binder (Capote)
Holds the filler leaves together in the bunch. Must be elastic enough to stretch and strong enough to hold its shape during the rolling process. Often contributes body, combustion quality, and structural integrity. The binder is chosen as much for burning properties as for flavor.
Filler (Tripa)
The blend of 2–5 leaves that make up the interior. The ligador (blender) uses leaves from different primings, regions, and fermentation stages to achieve a specific flavor profile, strength, and burn rate. Different filler leaves serve different functions: volado for combustion, seco for balance, ligero for strength and complexity.
The three filler primings
VOLADO
Lower priming — combustion leaf
Grows at the bottom of the plant. Lowest nicotine content. Burns exceptionally well. Used to ensure consistent combustion throughout the cigar. Mild flavor contribution but essential structural role.
SECO
Middle priming — flavor leaf
Mid-plant. Medium nicotine. Good combustion with complex flavor. Cedar, earth, spice, and sweetness notes. The backbone of most blends — contributes balance and body without dominating strength.
LIGERO
Top priming — strength leaf
Highest on the plant. Receives the most sunlight. Highest nicotine and oil content. Burns slowly — always placed in the center of the bunch so the cigar draws evenly. Used sparingly. Responsible for strength, body, and the long finish. Must be aged longer than other primings.
Wrapper colors
Double Claro (Candela)
Pale green to light tan. Dried rapidly with heat to preserve chlorophyll. Grassy, vegetal, and mild. Largely fallen out of fashion but still produced. Extremely low nicotine. Also called "AMS" (American Market Selection) from their mid-20th century popularity.
Claro — Very Light
Shade-grown under tent cloth. Light tan to golden. Extremely fine veins. Mild, smooth, and creamy with subtle sweetness. Ecuadorian Connecticut wrappers are the modern benchmark for this category — silky texture, elegant draw.
Natural (Colorado Claro)
Light to medium brown. Often sun-grown. The classic presentation for many Honduran and Dominican blends. Cedar, hay, light nuts, mild spice. More body than Claro but retains approachability.
Colorado — Medium Brown
The most common wrapper shade globally. Rich reddish-brown. Cedar, leather, earth, baking spice, dried fruit. Typical of Nicaraguan puro blends and many Cuban Habanos. Versatile across all strength profiles.
Colorado Maduro
Dark reddish-brown to deep brown. Heavier fermentation than Colorado. Richer, more complex — dark chocolate, coffee, and dried fruit with a natural sweetness. A transition color between Colorado and full Maduro.
Maduro — Very Dark
Fermented for months at high temperatures. Deep chocolate to almost black. The complex fermentation process breaks down harsh compounds, resulting in natural sweetness. Deep chocolate, espresso, dark fruit, earth. Oily sheen. One of the most misunderstood wrappers — not inherently strong, just rich.
Oscuro — Black
The darkest possible wrapper. Extended fermentation beyond Maduro. Very oily. Deep, intense flavors — dark chocolate, espresso, black pepper, molasses. Produced in limited quantities. Nicaraguan and Honduran ligero leaves are most commonly used. Not for the faint-hearted.
Cultivation methods
Shade-grown (Tapado)
Grown under cheesecloth or muslin tenting that filters sunlight by 30–50%. Produces the most prized wrapper leaves. Without direct sun stress, the leaf grows larger, thinner, and with fewer, finer veins. More elastic — easier to roll without cracking. Less oily and lower nicotine than sun-grown, which contributes to milder, creamier flavor profiles. Ecuador's persistent cloud cover naturally mimics this effect.
Sun-grown (Corojo / Criollo)
Grown in full sunlight. The leaf develops more oils and thicker veins as a defense against direct sun. Higher oil and nicotine content = more robust, complex, and spicy flavors. Nicaraguan sun-grown Corojo is the benchmark for full-bodied, pepper-forward wrappers. Historically, all Cuban wrapper tobacco was sun-grown.
Fermentation
After harvesting and initial drying, all tobacco goes through fermentation — a controlled composting process where internal heat breaks down ammonia, harsh compounds, and chlorophyll. Wrapper leaves may ferment multiple times over 1–3 years. Maduro fermentation is the most intense, often using steam, pressure, and temperatures up to 120°F. Proper fermentation is what separates fine premium tobacco from harsh commercial cigarette tobacco.
Notable wrapper varieties
Connecticut Shade
The definitive mild wrapper. Originally grown in the Connecticut River Valley; today most come from Ecuador under similar growing conditions. Silky, paper-thin, light golden. Creamy, nutty, slightly sweet. Used on many of the world's most approachable premium cigars.
Habano (Cuban-seed)
Cuban seed tobacco grown outside Cuba — Nicaragua, Ecuador, Honduras, Dominican Republic. Retains the genetic profile of Criollo and Corojo varieties that made Cuban tobacco famous. Spicy, complex, cedar, leather, pepper. The backbone of many of Nicaragua's most celebrated blends.
Sumatra
From the Indonesian island of Sumatra. Medium brown, fine texture, low to medium strength. Earthy, woody, slightly spicy with underlying sweetness. Used as both wrapper and binder in Dutch-style cigars and increasingly in New World blends for its consistency.
San Andrés (Mexican Maduro)
Grown in the San Andrés Tuxtla valley of Veracruz, Mexico. The gold standard for Maduro wrappers. Exceptionally dark, oily, and naturally sweet. Chocolate, coffee, dark fruit, molasses. Aged longer than most other Maduros. Used in iconic blends like Padron 1964 Maduro.
Corojo (Original Cuban)
The original premier Cuban wrapper variety, once grown exclusively in the Vuelta Abajo region of Cuba. Post-revolution seeds were smuggled to Honduras, Nicaragua, and Ecuador. Rich red-brown color, intense oils, bold pepper and spice. A connoisseur's wrapper.

What is Torcida

Torcida is your AI-powered cigar companion — built for everyone from the first-time smoker to the seasoned collector. Search any cigar for rich tasting notes and pairing recommendations, track your humidor, log every smoke, and explore the craft through a beautifully designed reference guide.

One app. Every part of the ritual.

Why We Are Called Torcida

Torcida is the feminine past participle of "to roll" in Spanish and Portuguese — a deliberate tribute to the women whose skilled hands have built the premium cigar industry for generations. Their names are rarely on the bands, but without them, there is no cigar.
